What to Look For When Hiring a Painting Contractor

February 10, 2026 · By [Author Name] · 6 min read

Painting looks straightforward from the outside. Tape, roll, done. That's what makes it such a common area for shortcuts — and why the gap between a good contractor and a bad one shows up years later as peeling trim, bleed-through stains, and a kitchen ceiling you have to redo before it's ready to. The upfront cost of the job is often similar. The difference lives in preparation, materials, and accountability.

Here's the checklist we'd give a friend before they hand a stranger a key to the house.

Start with license and insurance — both, in writing

A legitimate painting contractor will carry a current contractor's license (where required by state), general liability insurance, and workers' compensation coverage for their crew. Ask for copies. Any reputable company will send them without hesitation. If a contractor hedges, changes the subject, or tells you they "don't really need" any of these, that's the end of the conversation.

This matters more than homeowners realize. If an uninsured worker falls off a ladder on your property, you can become personally liable. A certificate of insurance takes five minutes to request and protects you from a potentially catastrophic outcome.

Ask for recent local references — not just Google reviews

Online reviews are useful, but they're also easy to curate. Ask for two or three references from jobs completed in the last six months in your area. Call them. Ask: Did the crew show up when they said they would? Did they protect the furniture and landscaping? Were there surprises on the final invoice? Would you hire them again?

A contractor who can't produce three recent local references is either new, hiding something, or not doing enough work to stay sharp. All three are problems.

Read the prep plan carefully

Paint is only as good as the surface underneath it. A top-tier contractor will write out what prep is included: power washing, scraping, sanding, caulking, priming of bare spots, patching of holes, and protection of floors and landscaping. A cut-rate contractor will give you one line: "Prep surfaces as needed." That vagueness is where the shortcuts hide.

Know the paint that's going on the wall

"We'll use premium paint" is not a specification. A proper quote names the brand, line, sheen, and number of coats for each surface. Two coats of a quality acrylic on properly primed walls behaves very differently than one coat of contractor-grade on bare drywall. If the contractor won't commit to specifics in writing, that's a tell.

Get everything in a written scope of work

The written scope should cover: exactly which surfaces are included (and which aren't), prep steps, paint product and sheen, color selections, number of coats, timeline, crew size, payment schedule, and the cleanup and walkthrough process. A deposit is normal. A demand for a large upfront cash payment is not. Most reputable painters work on a modest deposit with the balance due on completion, or a split across start and finish.

Red flags that should end the conversation

  • Pressure to decide today, or a "this price only if you sign now" pitch
  • Cash-only payment demands or refusal to provide a written contract
  • No physical business address, no local phone number, no presence online
  • Quote that's dramatically lower than everyone else's — someone is cutting a corner
  • Refusal to share proof of license, insurance, or recent references
  • Vague answers about warranty, product, or what happens if something goes wrong

Ask about the warranty

A confident painter will stand behind their work — typically somewhere between one and five years on labor, with the paint manufacturer's warranty handling the product itself. Ask what the warranty covers, how to make a claim, and what's specifically excluded. A two-line warranty on the back of the invoice is better than a verbal promise but weaker than a written multi-year guarantee.

The final walkthrough

Before you sign off on payment, walk the job with the contractor in good light. Look for drips, holidays (thin spots), overspray on windows or fixtures, and paint where it shouldn't be. Reputable painters expect this walkthrough and will happily fix anything you flag. If your contractor rushes you, pushes back, or calls minor defects "just how paint looks" — they knew, and they were hoping you wouldn't notice.

Hiring a painter is ultimately a trust decision. The checklists above aren't a substitute for instinct — they're a way to verify that your instinct is reading the right signals. When the paperwork, the conversation, and the references all line up, you can relax and let the pros do what they do best.
